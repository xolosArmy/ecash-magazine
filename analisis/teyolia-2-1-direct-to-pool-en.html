Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool: the trustless route to bring XEC directly into native THORChain liquidity

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is technically viable as an architecture, but not as a simple adjustment to Teyolia 2.0. To work correctly, it needs a new finalization contract version, a strict late-binding policy for THORChain vaults, and an explicit definition of who will own the resulting LP position.

MG Technical Team June 2026 Teyolia · THORChain · eCash

The core idea behind Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is simple to describe but delicate to implement: allow a community campaign on eCash to accumulate XEC and, once it reaches its target, send the funds directly into the native XEC/RUNE pool on THORChain, without passing through an intermediate custodian.

The technical conclusion is clear: it is worth moving forward, but only as a new architecture. Direct-to-Pool should not be treated as “changing the beneficiary to a THORChain address.” It must be a new finalization path with verifiable rules, real-time preflight checks, and strict compatibility with how THORChain processes UTXO transactions.

The zero condition remains external: XEC must be officially supported by THORNode/Bifrost and must appear in inbound_addresses with production parameters. In THORChain’s public list of supported chains, eCash/XEC does not yet appear, and the public inbound_addresses endpoint observed lists chains such as BTC, BCH, DOGE, LTC, ETH, AVAX, BASE, SOL, TRON and XRP, but not XEC.

Precondition 0: XEC must first exist on THORChain mainnet

Before talking about production, two separate layers must be distinguished.

At the design layer, Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is viable in principle. eCash can build UTXO transactions, carry a short memo and sign locally from a wallet. THORChain, for its part, already has a known semantic model for UTXO deposits: send to an inbound vault, include a memo and let the nodes observe the transaction.

But at the operational layer, Teyolia 2.1 cannot function until XEC is merged, deployed and publicly exposed as a supported chain by THORChain. THORChain’s own documentation indicates that inbound transactions must retrieve the active vault from the inbound_addresses endpoint, check whether the network is halted, and use the recommended gas_rate before sending funds.

Therefore, the correct version of this thesis is not “Teyolia 2.1 is ready for production,” but rather:

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is prepared as an architectural specification for implementation once the required infrastructure preconditions are met.

What changes compared with Teyolia 2.0

Teyolia 2.0 already introduces an important improvement over a classic custodial scheme: the campaign exists on eCash, the user signs from their wallet, and the backend acts as a builder or coordinator, not as the natural owner of the funds.

Teyolia 2.1 pushes that logic further.

The difference is not only the destination of the XEC. The difference is who controls the final hop.

In Teyolia 2.0, the flow may still depend on operators or a backend to complete the output toward the final objective. In Teyolia 2.1, the idea is that the finalization contract itself limits what kind of transaction can close the campaign. This means the contract should no longer simply say “pay the beneficiary,” but rather “only allow finalization if the transaction has a THORChain-compatible shape.”

That change requires a contract version bump. It is not a configuration parameter. It is new semantics.

The correct architecture: accumulation on eCash, just-in-time finalization

The recommended architecture has two layers.

The first is eCash. That is where users contribute XEC to a campaign with clear rules: target, expiration, finalization mode, refund policy and contract version.

The second is THORChain. That layer should only activate at the moment the campaign is closed. Not before.

The reason is critical: THORChain inbound vault addresses are not permanent addresses. The documentation explicitly warns that they should not be cached because they change with churn, and that inbound transactions should not be delayed in order to avoid sending funds to an obsolete or unreachable address.

The correct pattern would be:

1. Target The campaign reaches its target on eCash.
2. Preflight The builder queries the current inbound_addresses entry for XEC.
3. Mimir Check It checks halted status, paused actions, gas_rate and dust.
4. Build v2.1 It builds a THORChain-compatible finalize transaction.
5. Local Sign The wallet signs locally, preserving the non-custodial model.
6. Just-in-Time Broadcast The transaction is broadcast immediately.

If it does not enter the network within a reasonable window, it must be rebuilt with fresh data. The golden rule is this:

The campaign must not commit to a THORChain vault at creation time. It must commit to a valid finalization shape.

The transaction shape THORChain needs

For UTXO chains, THORChain requires a specific structure. The documentation indicates that the amount must go to the Asgard vault as VOUT0, change must return to the VIN0 address, the memo must go in OP_RETURN, normally as VOUT2, and the transaction must not exceed 10 outputs.

Translated into Teyolia 2.1, a Direct-to-Pool finalization would need to look like this:

VOUT0
XEC to the current THORChain inbound vault.
VOUT1
Change to VIN0, if applicable.
VOUT2
OP_RETURN with ADD memo.
VOUT3+
Additional fragments or infrastructure treasury output.

The memo for adding liquidity on THORChain uses the form:

ADD:POOL:PAIREDADDR:AFFILIATE:FEE

The short alias + can also be used. THORChain documents that PAIREDADDR is optional, that it can link the other side of the deposit, and that if it is not specified, a single-sided liquidity action is created.

For Teyolia, the safest memo profiles would be:

+:XEC.XEC

for a minimal test, or:

+:XEC.XEC:<thor-address-owner>

if the operational owner of the LP position should be explicitly defined. A memo with a protocol fee could look like this:

+:XEC.XEC:<thor-address-owner>:<affiliate-thorname>:100

But that option should only be used if THORChain confirms the exact affiliate-fee semantics for ADDLP in the XEC case.

Short memo or nothing

One of the most important points is not to confuse two different limits.

On the BCH/eCash side, the Bitcoin Cash-style script family allows broader data outputs than Bitcoin Core. The Bitcoin Cash technical reference describes a standard OP_RETURN with a maximum length of 223 bytes, and eCash keeps a base unit with two decimals, equivalent to 100 satoshis per XEC.

But for Direct-to-Pool, what matters is not only what eCash can carry. What matters is what THORChain will process safely.

THORChain’s standard UTXO path works with memos of up to 80 characters in OP_RETURN. Although there is a strategy for longer memos through additional outputs, the safe design for Teyolia 2.1 should be conservative:

Every production memo must fit within THORChain’s short memo path.

That means avoiding designs that depend on advanced memo reconstruction, unnecessary long addresses or excessive metadata. Teyolia 2.1 does not need a long memo to prove sovereignty. It needs a correct memo.

The central problem: who will own the LP position?

The most delicate question is not technical but architectural: who will be able to withdraw or manage the liquidity afterward?

THORChain explains that liquidity provision is permissionless and non-custodial, but also that only the original depositor can withdraw their share of the pool. It also distinguishes between symmetric and asymmetric deposits; in asymmetric deposits, the system ultimately exposes the LP to both RUNE and the external asset, with slip depending on pool depth.

This creates a critical decision for Teyolia:

If the mode is XEC-only asymmetric, the UX is simpler: the campaign collects XEC and sends it directly to the pool. But the position may remain associated with the source of the closing transaction, not necessarily with a community identity that is easy to manage.

If the mode is dual-sided or pending dual-sided, the memo can link the XEC deposit with a THOR address controlled by a community entity, multisig or governance scheme. Then, a second RUNE leg completes the bootstrap. It is more complex, but it solves future LP ownership more cleanly from a governance perspective.

Therefore, Direct-to-Pool should not be a single feature. It should have at least two profiles:

Teyolia 2.1-A
XEC-only asymmetric Direct-to-Pool.
Teyolia 2.1-B
Pending dual-sided Direct-to-Pool: XEC + RUNE.

The first variant is useful to demonstrate flow and protocol liquidity. The second is better for a manageable position and cleaner governance.

The 1% fee: two paths, two philosophies

The 1% fee should not be treated as an administrative detail. It must be part of the verifiable design.

There are two main routes.

1% as protocol LP

The first route is to use THORChain’s native affiliate fee in the memo. The affiliate-fee guide explains that 100 bps equals 1%, that a valid THORName is required to collect fees, and that when the affiliate is added to an ADDLP transaction, the affiliate is included as an LP.

Advantage: it is elegant, atomic and aligned with THORChain. The fee only exists if the protocol correctly processes the ADD.

Disadvantage: it does not necessarily work as immediately liquid XEC treasury. It may be expressed as a position or flow inside THORChain.

1% as liquid XEC treasury

The second route is to perform an L1 split:

VOUT0
99% to the inbound vault.
VOUT1
Change to VIN0.
VOUT2
OP_RETURN with memo.
VOUT3
1% to the XEC treasury.

Advantage: it is transparent on eCash, easy to audit and useful for day-to-day operations.

Risk: if THORChain rejects, delays or refunds the VOUT0 deposit, VOUT3 has already delivered the fee irreversibly.

Therefore, if Teyolia uses an L1 split, the failure mode must be designed into the contract. It cannot be an extra output added “just because.”

Risks and mitigations

The first risk is external: XEC still needs to reach official support in THORNode/Bifrost. Without inbound_addresses for XEC, without dust_threshold, without gas_rate and without final address formats, there is no real operational target.

The second risk is contractual: the current finalize path should not be reused as if it were equivalent. Direct-to-Pool needs contractVersion = 2.1, unit tests, reconstruction tests and explicit output rules.

The third risk is ownership. If the LP is associated with the wrong identity, the liquidity can become difficult to manage or practically unrecoverable.

The fourth risk is operational: vaults change, the network can pause LP actions, and halted states or chain_lp_actions_paused must be respected. THORChain documentation warns that if the network is halted, funds should not be sent, and that interfaces must observe Mimir controls to avoid unsupported actions.

The fifth risk is economic: an asymmetric bootstrap in a new or shallow pool can generate high slip and immediate arbitrage. THORChain recognizes that asymmetric deposits have slip impact and that LPs are often incentivized to deposit symmetrically when seeking a more balanced launch.

Validation status with THORChain

The technical conversation with the THORChain team helped close one of the most important design questions: the Layer 1 return path. In a dual-sided LP flow, the THOR address can initiate the withdrawal, but the XEC side returns to the paired L1 address that THORChain considers the sender of the initial ADD. For that reason, Teyolia 2.1 cannot treat an ephemeral covenant as the secure owner of a manageable LP position.

From that clarification, some questions stop being open questions and become architecture decisions:

  1. Long memo: discarded as a production dependency. Teyolia 2.1 must use short memos compatible with THORChain’s standard UTXO path.
  2. Ownership: separated into two parts: the THOR address can initiate withdrawal, but XEC must return to a durable and recoverable L1 address.
  3. Output ordering: must follow THORChain’s UTXO pattern: VOUT0 inbound vault, change to VIN0, OP_RETURN with memo, and additional outputs only if they do not break limits or failure modes.

However, several points still need to be validated before a production implementation:

  1. Final confirmation of XEC support in THORNode/Bifrost mainnet.
  2. Production values for dust_threshold, gas_rate, address formats and halted status for XEC in inbound_addresses.
  3. Exact semantics of the affiliate fee in ADDLP for XEC.
  4. Availability of an official quote or estimate for adding XEC liquidity from day one.

Implementing Sovereignty

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool can be a structural improvement over Teyolia 2.0, but only if it is formulated as a new finalization architecture.

This is not about sending funds “to a THORChain address.” It is about correctly encoding the final hop: which outputs are valid, which memo is allowed, when the current vault is queried, how the fee is handled, who will own the LP and what happens if THORChain is not ready to process the deposit.

Direct-to-Pool must not hardcode a vault. It must hardcode a rule of sovereignty.

Teyolia 2.1 will not be more trustless because it promises that funds go directly into the pool. It will be more trustless if the contract prevents them from going anywhere else, if the builder only acts as an assistant, if the wallet signs locally and if the final state can be verified from the chain.

XEC is the money.
Liquidity must be native.
And the final hop cannot depend on trust: it must be written into the contract.
