Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool: The trustless route to bring XEC directly to native liquidity on THORChain

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is technically viable as an architecture, but not as a simple tweak to Teyolia 2.0. To function correctly, it requires a new version of the finalization contract, a strict late binding policy for THORChain vaults, and an explicit definition of who will own the resulting LP position.

MG Technical Team June 2026 Teyolia · THORChain · eCash

The central idea of Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is simple to state, but delicate to implement: allowing a community campaign on eCash to accumulate XEC and, upon reaching its goal, send the funds directly to the native XEC/RUNE pool on THORChain, without going through intermediate custody.

The technical conclusion is clear: it is worth advancing, but only as a new architecture. Direct-to-Pool shouldn't be "changing the beneficiary to a THORChain address." It must be a new finalization route with verifiable rules, real-time preflight, and strict compatibility with how THORChain processes UTXO transactions.

The zero condition remains external: XEC must be officially supported by THORNode/Bifrost and appear in inbound_addresses with production parameters. On the public list of chains supported by THORChain, eCash/XEC does not appear yet, and the queried public inbound_addresses endpoint shows chains like BTC, BCH, DOGE, LTC, ETH, AVAX, BASE, SOL, TRON, and XRP, but not XEC.

Precondition 0: XEC must first exist on THORChain mainnet

Before talking about production, two planes must be separated.

On the design plane, Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is viable in principle. eCash can construct UTXO transactions, transport a short memo, and sign locally from a wallet. THORChain, on the other hand, already has a known semantics for UTXO deposits: send to an incoming vault, include a memo, and let the nodes observe the transaction.

But on the operational plane, Teyolia 2.1 cannot function until XEC is merged, deployed, and publicly exposed as a supported chain by THORChain. THORChain's own documentation indicates that inbound transactions must take the active vault from the inbound_addresses endpoint, check if halted, and use the recommended gas_rate before sending funds.

Therefore, the correct version of this thesis is not "Teyolia 2.1 is ready for production", but rather:

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool is prepared as an architectural specification for implementation once the infrastructure preconditions are met.

What changes compared to Teyolia 2.0

Teyolia 2.0 already poses a significant improvement over a classic custodial scheme: the campaign exists on eCash, the user signs from their wallet, and the backend operates as a builder or coordinator, not as the natural owner of the funds.

Teyolia 2.1 pushes that logic further.

The difference is not just in the destination of the XEC. The difference lies in who controls the final hop.

In Teyolia 2.0, the flow may still depend on operators or the backend to complete the exit toward the final goal. In Teyolia 2.1, the idea is that the finalization contract itself limits what kind of transaction can close the campaign. This means the contract should no longer simply say "pay the beneficiary", but "only allow finalization if the transaction has a form compatible with THORChain."

That change requires a contract version bump. It is not a configuration parameter. It is a new semantic.

The correct architecture: accumulation on eCash, just-in-time finalization

The recommended architecture has two planes.

The first is eCash. There, users contribute XEC to a campaign with clear rules: goal, expiration, finalization mode, refund policy, and contract version.

The second is THORChain. That plane should only activate at the moment of closing the campaign. Not before.

The reason is critical: THORChain's inbound vault addresses are not permanent addresses. The documentation explicitly warns that they should not be cached because they change with churn, and that inbound transactions should not be delayed to avoid sending funds to an obsolete or unreachable address.

The correct pattern would be:

1. Goal The campaign reaches its goal on eCash.
2. Preflight Builder queries current inbound_addresses for XEC.
3. Verify Mimir Verify halted, paused, gas_rate and dust.
4. Build v2.1 Builds the finalize transaction compatible with THORChain.
5. Sign Local The wallet signs locally (non-custodial).
6. Transmit Just-in-Time The transaction is transmitted immediately.

If it doesn't get mined in a reasonable window, it is rebuilt with fresh data. The golden rule is this:

The campaign must not commit to a THORChain vault at the time of creation. It must commit to a valid form of finalization.

The transaction shape that THORChain requires

For UTXO chains, THORChain requires a specific structure. The documentation indicates that the amount must go to the Asgard vault as VOUT0, the change must return to the VIN0 address, the memo must go in OP_RETURN, usually as VOUT2, and the transaction must not exceed 10 outputs.

Translated to Teyolia 2.1, a Direct-to-Pool finalization would have to look like this:

VOUT0
XEC toward current THORChain inbound vault.
VOUT1
Change toward VIN0, if applicable.
VOUT2
OP_RETURN with ADD memo.
VOUT3+
Additional fragments or infrastructure treasury.

The memo to add liquidity on THORChain uses the form:

ADD:POOL:PAIREDADDR:AFFILIATE:FEE

The short alias + can also be used. THORChain documents that PAIREDADDR is optional, allowing the linking of the other address of the deposit, and if not specified, it creates a single-sided liquidity action.

For Teyolia, the safest memo profiles would be:

+:XEC.XEC

for a minimal test, or:

+:XEC.XEC:<thor-address-owner>

if you want to explicitly define the operational owner of the LP position. A memo with a protocolary fee might look like this:

+:XEC.XEC:<thor-address-owner>:<affiliate-thorname>:100

But that option should only be used if THORChain confirms the exact semantics of the affiliate fee for ADDLP in the XEC case.

Short memo or nothing

One of the most important points is not to confuse two different limits.

On the BCH/eCash side, the Bitcoin Cash style script family allows broader data outputs than Bitcoin Core. The technical reference for Bitcoin Cash describes a standard OP_RETURN with a maximum length of 223 bytes, and eCash maintains a base unit with two decimals, equivalent to 100 satoshis per XEC.

But for Direct-to-Pool, what matters is not just what eCash can transport. What matters is what THORChain will process securely.

The standard UTXO route in THORChain works with memos of up to 80 characters in OP_RETURN. Although there is a strategy for longer memos through additional outputs, the secure design for Teyolia 2.1 must be conservative:

Every production memo must fit into the short THORChain route.

That means avoiding designs that rely on advanced memo reconstruction, unnecessarily long addresses, or excessive metadata. Teyolia 2.1 doesn't need a long memo to prove sovereignty. It needs a correct memo.

The core issue: who will own the LP position

The most delicate issue is not technical but architectural: who will be able to withdraw or manage the liquidity later?

THORChain explains that providing liquidity is permissionless and non-custodial, but also that only the original depositor can withdraw their participation from the pool. It also distinguishes between symmetrical and asymmetrical deposits; in asymmetrical ones, the system ends up exposing the LP to both RUNE and the external asset, with slip depending on the pool's depth.

This creates a critical decision for Teyolia:

If the mode is asymmetrical XEC-only, the UX is simpler: the campaign gathers XEC and sends it straight to the pool. But the position may remain associated with the origin of the closing transaction and not necessarily with an easy-to-manage community identity.

If the mode is dual-sided or pending dual-sided, the memo can link the XEC deposit with a THOR address controlled by a community entity, multisig, or governance scheme. Then, a second leg in RUNE completes the bootstrap. It is more complex, but it better resolves the future ownership of the LP.

Therefore, Direct-to-Pool shouldn't be a single feature. It should have at least two profiles:

Teyolia 2.1-A
Direct-to-Pool asymmetrical XEC-only.
Teyolia 2.1-B
Direct-to-Pool pending dual-sided XEC + RUNE.

The first variant serves to demonstrate flow and protocol liquidity. The second serves for a manageable position, cleaner from a governance standpoint.

The 1% fee: two paths, two philosophies

The 1% fee should not be treated as an administrative detail. It must be part of the verifiable design.

There are two main routes.

1% as protocolary LP

The first is to use THORChain's native affiliate fee in the memo. The affiliate fees guide explains that 100 bps equals 1%, that a valid THORName is required to collect fees, and that when the affiliate is added to an ADDLP transaction, the affiliate is included as an LP.

Advantage: It is elegant, atomic, and aligned with THORChain. The fee only exists if the protocol correctly processes the ADD.

Disadvantage: It does not necessarily function as an immediate liquid treasury in XEC. It may remain expressed as a position or flow within THORChain.

1% as liquid treasury in XEC

The second route is to do an L1 split:

VOUT0
99% to inbound vault.
VOUT1
Change to VIN0.
VOUT2
OP_RETURN with memo.
VOUT3
1% to XEC treasury.

Advantage: It is transparent on eCash, easy to audit, and useful for daily operations.

Risk: If THORChain rejects, delays, or refunds the VOUT0 deposit, VOUT3 has already delivered the fee irreversibly.

Therefore, if Teyolia uses an L1 split, the failure mode must be designed into the contract. It cannot be an additional output "just because."

Risks and mitigations

The first risk is external: XEC still must achieve official support in THORNode/Bifrost. Without inbound_addresses for XEC, without dust_threshold, without gas_rate, and without final address formats, there is no real operational target.

The second risk is contractual: the current finalize must not be reused as if it were equivalent. Direct-to-Pool requires contractVersion = 2.1, unit testing, reconstruction testing, and explicit output rules.

The third risk is ownership. If the LP remains associated with the wrong identity, liquidity can become difficult to manage or practically unrecoverable.

The fourth risk is operational: vaults change, the network can pause LP actions, and halted or chain_lp_actions_paused states must be respected. THORChain documentation warns that if the network is halted, funds must not be sent, and that interfaces must observe Mimir controls to avoid unsupported actions.

The fifth risk is economic: an asymmetrical bootstrap in a new or shallow pool can generate high slip and immediate arbitrage. THORChain acknowledges that asymmetrical deposits have an impact due to slip and that LPs are usually incentivized to deposit symmetrically when seeking a more balanced start.

Open questions for THORChain

Before moving from specification to implementation, six points should be validated with the THORChain technical team:

  1. What will be the actual status of XEC in THORNode/Bifrost and what acceptance criteria will it have for mainnet?
  2. Will the long memo parser for BCH-style routes work the same for XEC, or will the only safe path be an 80-character short memo?
  3. In an ADD originating from an eCash covenant/script, what exact identity is registered as the original depositor of the LP position?
  4. In ADDLP, does the 1% affiliate fee always remain as the affiliate's LP position or is there any liquid distribution route comparable to swaps?
  5. What will be the final parameters for dust_threshold, output ordering, max outputs, and address formats for the XEC chain client?
  6. Will there be an official quote or estimate to add liquidity in XEC from day one?

Implementing Sovereignty

Teyolia 2.1 Direct-to-Pool can be a structural improvement over Teyolia 2.0, but only if it is formulated as a new finalization architecture.

It's not about sending funds "to a THORChain address." It's about correctly coding the final hop: what outputs are valid, what memo is allowed, when the current vault is queried, how the fee is handled, who will own the LP, and what happens if THORChain is not ready to process the deposit.

Direct-to-Pool must not hardcode a vault. It must hardcode a sovereignty rule.

Teyolia 2.1 won't be more trustless by promising that funds go directly to the pool. It will be more trustless if the contract prevents them from going anywhere else, if the builder acts only as an assistant, if the wallet signs locally, and if the final state can be verified on-chain.

XEC is the money.
Liquidity must be native.
And the final hop cannot rely on trust: it must be written in the contract.
